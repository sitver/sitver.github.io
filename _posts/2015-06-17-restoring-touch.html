We're one step closer to Terminator...

For the first time, paralyzed patients can "feel" things they touch with their brain-controlled robotic arm. Scientists at The University of Chicago have developed a brain implant that turns sensor data from robotic arms into "feeling" in the brain, by using electricity to stimulate the somatosensory cortex of the brain. 

After years of research and testing, a month ago a tetraplegic patient received the first of these touch-restoring implants.

Touch is not the first sense to be restored by the ever-growing field of  "neuro-prosthetics", electronic devices that not only mimic human functions, but interface with the body:

Cochlear implants, which work by recording sound, converting it into electric signals, and passing it on to the auditory nerve, have restored hearing to 300,000 patients since their introduction. Visual neuro-prosthetics in trial stages are starting to restore functional sight to blind patients. 

But this discovery is so much bigger...

Right now, anyone who is tetraplegic requires constant care. They cannot walk, or grab things, or dress themselves. This places a huge burden of cost and time on their families, and places a huge psychological burden, guilt, on those who suffer from this.

Neuro-prosthetic arms allow these patients to do some things for themselves, and may eventually allow them to live independently. 

Why is the sensation touch so important? Without the sensation of touch , artificial limbs have been very difficult to control. Studies show that even basic tasks like lighting a match can be very difficult, if the thumb and forefinger are numbed. 

That's why this advancement is so huge. With the sensation of touch, patients may one day be able to complete difficult, pressure-sensitive tasks such as painting, or gripping an egg.

It took a lot of time and effort, even after finding the technology to be feasible, to get to this point. Scientists had to find the electrical signals that mimicked the sensation of touch. They had to find a way to map pressure sensors to electrical signals, and convert any level of pressure into a feeling, and they needed to make sure it was safe. 

The question is no longer whether it's feasible. The question is how far this technology can go. Dr. Sliman Bensmaia, one of the lead researchers on this project for The University of Chicago, believes that one day robotic arms could surpass the human arm in terms of strength, responsiveness,  and speed.

But that's down the road. For now, we'll just have to settle for restoring autonomy to the lives of a few paraplegic folks. That's pretty good.
